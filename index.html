Design Philosophy

🎮

Progressive Disclosure

Every mechanic deserves its own moment. I design levels where each new rule is introduced in isolation — maximum simplicity first, complexity earned through play. Players should never feel overwhelmed, always feel capable.

🧩

Constraint as Design

Locked slots, blocked colors, and routing bottlenecks aren't punishments — they're the puzzle. Removing freedom of movement forces players to find the one elegant path that exists, transforming frustration into satisfaction.

📊

Data-Driven Iteration

Level 17 in Thread Cats has a 68% first-attempt fail rate — by design. I use playtest data to calibrate skill gates precisely: challenging enough to validate mastery, never punishing enough to cause drop-off.

⚙️

Systems Over Scripts

The best game moments emerge from systems colliding, not scripted events. I design mechanics that interact with each other — each element amplifying the difficulty of others without ever adding new rules.

// DESIGN_NOTES · PLAYTEST_RESULTS · MECHANIC_RETROSPECTIVES

2024 // THREAD CATS LEVEL DESIGN ONBOARDING
Designing a Zero-Friction Tutorial: Levels 1–4 Retrospective
The core challenge of Thread Cats was making color-as-logic feel intuitive before adding the gap mechanic. Early playtests showed players treating color as aesthetic rather than functional — they'd try any slot, not the right one. The solution was radical constraint: 4 colors maximum, 4 open slots, no visual noise. By limiting the solution space completely, we forced players to observe the color-to-slot relationship without realising they were learning a rule. By level 3, players were applying the logic unconsciously. The tube mechanic tutorial was introduced only after color logic was internalised — a deliberate sequencing decision that paid off significantly.
RESULT // Levels 1–4 completed without hints by 94% of new players in playtest cohort
2024 // THREAD CATS SKILL GATE PLAYTEST DATA
Level 17: Engineering the Perfect Skill Validation Checkpoint
Level 17 exists to answer one question: does the player understand the system, or are they pattern-matching? The level was designed with multiple misleading paths — routes that look valid but lead to dead ends. The only correct solution requires reading the board fully before the first move. Initial versions had a 45% fail rate, too low to act as a meaningful gate. We added a secondary misleading path and tightened the slot count, pushing the rate to 68%. The key insight: a skill gate should feel beatable on second attempt, not on first. Players who fail and retry are internalising the system; players who never fail aren't learning.
RESULT // 68% first-attempt fail rate. Zero drop-off at this level in final playtest cohort.
2024 // LIGHT BULBS BLOCK PUZZLE MECHANICS HARD MODE
Frozen Blocks: Designing a Two-Phase Level Within One Level
The frozen block mechanic emerged from a design problem: how do you extend level depth without adding new rules? The answer was state — blocks that exist in the level but aren't yet active. Frozen blocks require a surrounding clear count before they unlock, creating a meta-puzzle within the main level: clear enough mass to reveal the secondary challenge, then solve whatever that reveals. Combined with a shortened timer and stone tiles fragmenting the board, Hard mode transforms the same core mechanic into a time-pressured routing problem. The player is no longer finding a solution — they're finding the fastest one under compounding constraints.
RESULT // Hard mode retention was 23% higher than projected. Players cited "double twist" as the most satisfying feature.
2024 // MOB JAM DECISION DESIGN TUBE SYSTEM
Tube Selection as a Decision Puzzle, Not a Spatial One
Early Mob Jam designs treated tubes as spatial connectors — routes to clear for access. Playtests revealed players clearing tubes opportunistically, without anticipating queue consequences. The redesign reframed tubes as decision points: both tube clearances are valid moves, but they lead to fundamentally different board states. The correct choice depends entirely on what colour appears next in the enemy queue. This forced a behaviour change — players now read the queue before touching the board. The moment a player pauses to plan their tube choice based on queue state is the moment the game becomes strategic rather than reactive.
RESULT // Queue-reading behaviour detected in 71% of playtest sessions after tube redesign (vs 28% prior).
2024 // MOB JAM VISIBILITY UX ANALYSIS
Hidden "?" Slots: Soft Visibility Constraints and Planning Tension
The hidden slot mechanic in Mob Jam addresses a core tension in puzzle design: perfect information removes stakes, imperfect information creates anxiety. The "?" marker obscures unit color/type until the player chooses to reveal — a free action with a timing cost. Players who reveal early gain planning advantage; players who rush reveal nothing and react. This asymmetry rewards attention without punishing inaction directly. The key design constraint was ensuring that a late reveal never makes a level unsolvable — the "?" is a planning tool, not a trap. Stress is created through time pressure, not information denial.
RESULT // Players who revealed "?" blocks early had 34% higher first-attempt completion rate on affected levels.

ACT01
Encounter Scripting & Battle Scripts
// FPS · SCRIPTED_ENCOUNTERS · COMBAT_FLOW
▼

Battle scripts define the choreography of enemy behaviour within a level — spawn timing, patrol routes, aggro triggers, and fallback positions. Each script is authored with three difficulty variants: normal, hard, and a hidden speedrun route. The goal is a level that reads as natural to a new player while rewarding experienced ones who manipulate the script flow.

  • Trigger-based spawn scripting via UE4 Blueprints
  • Enemy group composition designed around player weapon loadout assumptions
  • Fallback and patrol behaviour for reactive engagement
  • Environment cues that telegraph enemy positions without breaking immersion

FPS level flow follows a tension-release cycle: tight corridors build pressure, open arenas release it.   • Tension-release pacing across linear and semi-open zones

Design Principles: Level Architecture

📐 Spatial Validation First

Every level is designed in 2D (Moqups/Figma) before any 3D or Blueprint work begins. Spatial flow problems are cheap to fix in wireframe; expensive in engine. The blueprint defines the experience arc — the 3D is art direction on top.

⚔️ Encounter Budgeting

Each encounter has a cognitive load budget. Too many variables at once causes decision paralysis. Enemy group compositions are designed so the player can read the room in 1–2 seconds: "two ranged, one heavy, one entry bottleneck." Clarity enables strategy.

🔄 Tension–Release Cycles

Long corridors compress tension; open arenas release it. A good level alternates these rhythmically, with the finale always in a larger space than the buildup. Pacing is as critical as combat balance — a well-paced level is memorable; a well-balanced but poorly-paced one is forgotten.

📖 Lore-Justified Constraints

Chokepoints and restricted access should never feel arbitrary. In Tech Darkness, every bottleneck is a facility design choice — locked security doors, collapsed infrastructure, server racks blocking corridors. The world justifies the design; the design serves the gameplay.
